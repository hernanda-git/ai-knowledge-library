## 1. Introduction

The AI revolution has created an entirely new category of jobs. These roles didn't exist five years ago and have rapidly evolved from niche positions to critical organizational functions. This document provides detailed profiles of the most important new AI job roles, including the skills required, salary expectations, career progression paths, and hiring trends.

### Why These Roles Matter

The emergence of new AI job roles reflects several fundamental shifts:

1. **AI Products Require Human Implementation**: AI tools need skilled humans to deploy, customize, and optimize them for specific use cases
2. **AI Creates New Risks**: Security, ethics, and governance challenges require dedicated specialists
3. **AI Changes How We Work**: New collaboration patterns between humans and AI systems require new job functions
4. **AI Generates New Data Challenges**: Synthetic data, AI training, and model evaluation create specialized roles

## 2. Forward Deployed Engineer

### 2.1 Role Definition

A Forward Deployed Engineer (FDE) is a hybrid technical role that combines software engineering skills with customer-facing implementation work. FDEs work directly with customers to deploy AI solutions, customize implementations, and ensure successful adoption.

**Why This Role Emerged:**
- AI products are complex and require significant customization
- Customers need hands-on help integrating AI into existing workflows
- The "last mile" of AI deployment is human-intensive
- FDEs drive customer retention and expansion revenue for AI companies

### 2.2 Skills Required

**Technical Skills:**
- Programming (Python, JavaScript, SQL)
- AI/ML fundamentals (understanding of models, fine-tuning, evaluation)
- API integration and system architecture
- Data engineering and ETL pipelines
- Cloud platforms (AWS, GCP, Azure)
- Version control and CI/CD

**Non-Technical Skills:**
- Customer communication and relationship building
- Solution architecture and problem-solving
- Project management and timeline coordination
- Technical writing and documentation
- Business acumen and ROI analysis
- Presentation and demo skills

### 2.3 Salary and Growth

| Level | Salary Range | Experience | Responsibilities |
|-------|-------------|------------|-----------------|
| Junior FDE | $110K-$140K | 0-2 years | Assist with implementations, learn customer workflows |
| Mid-Level FDE | $140K-$180K | 2-5 years | Lead customer implementations, handle complex integrations |
| Senior FDE | $180K-$230K | 5-8 years | Architect solutions, mentor junior FDEs, drive strategy |
| Principal FDE | $230K-$300K | 8+ years | Define FDE methodology, lead large-scale deployments |

**Growth Rate**: 340% (2024-2026)
**Demand**: Extremely high — AI companies are competing aggressively for FDEs

### 2.4 Career Progression

```
Junior FDE (0-2 years)
    ↓
Mid-Level FDE (2-5 years)
    ↓
Senior FDE (5-8 years)
    ↓
Principal FDE (8+ years)

```

**Alternative Paths:**
- FDE → Solutions Architect → CTO
- FDE → Product Manager → VP Product

### 2.5 Day-in-the-Life

**Monday-Wednesday:**
- Customer discovery calls to understand implementation requirements

- Test and validate implementations

**Thursday-Friday:**

## 3. AI Prompt Engineer

### 3.1 Role Definition

An AI Prompt Engineer designs, tests, and optimizes prompts for AI systems — particularly large language models. They bridge the gap between human intent and AI capability, ensuring that AI systems produce accurate, relevant, and useful outputs.

**Why This Role Emerged:**
- Prompt quality dramatically affects AI output quality
- Organizations need consistent, reproducible AI interactions
- Different use cases require different prompt strategies
- Prompt engineering is a learnable skill with measurable impact

### 3.2 Skills Required

**Technical Skills:**
- Deep understanding of LLM behavior and capabilities
- Prompt design patterns and techniques
- Few-shot learning and chain-of-thought prompting
- Evaluation methodology for AI outputs
- Basic programming (Python) for automation
- Understanding of token limits, context windows, and model parameters

**Non-Technical Skills:**
- Clear communication and instruction design
- Analytical thinking and debugging mindset
- Domain expertise in relevant fields
- Documentation and knowledge sharing

### 3.3 Salary and Growth

**Growth Rate**: 200% (2024-2026)
**Demand**: Very high, though some predict this role may evolve into broader AI interaction design

### 3.4 Prompt Engineering Framework

**The CREATE Framework:**
```
C - Context: Set the stage for the AI
R - Role: Assign the AI a specific role
E - Examples: Provide few-shot examples
A - Action: Clearly state the desired output
T - Type: Specify the format and style
E - Evaluation: Define success criteria
```

**Example Application:**
```
[Context] You are analyzing customer support tickets for a SaaS company.
[Role] Act as a senior customer success analyst.
[Examples] 
  Input: "Login broken on mobile" → Category: Technical/Bug, Priority: High
  Input: "How do I export data?" → Category: Support/Question, Priority: Low
[Action] Classify the following ticket by category and priority.
[Type] Return as JSON: {"category": "...", "priority": "...", "summary": "..."}
[Evaluation] Accuracy should be >90% on test set.
```

```
    ↓
Senior Prompt Engineer (4-7 years)
```

### 4.4 Governance Framework

**The AI Ethics Assessment Process:**
```
1. System Inventory
   - What AI systems are deployed?
   - What data do they use?
   - What decisions do they influence?

2. Risk Assessment
   - What are the potential harms?
   - Who could be affected?
   - What is the severity and likelihood?

3. Fairness Evaluation
   - Does the system perform equally across demographic groups?
   - Are there proxy variables causing indirect bias?
   - What fairness metrics apply?

4. Transparency Review
   - Can affected individuals understand how decisions are made?
   - Are AI outputs explainable?
   - Is there appropriate disclosure?

5. Compliance Check
   - Does the system comply with relevant regulations?
   - Are there required documentation and reporting obligations?
   - What audit trails are maintained?

6. Ongoing Monitoring
   - How is system performance tracked over time?
   - What triggers a re-evaluation?
   - How are incidents reported and addressed?
```

### 6.4 AI Threat Landscape

**Common AI Attack Vectors:**

| Attack Type | Description | Impact | Mitigation |
|------------|-------------|--------|------------|
| **Adversarial Examples** | Manipulated inputs that fool AI models | Incorrect decisions | Adversarial training, input validation |
| **Data Poisoning** | Corrupting training data to bias models | Biased/flawed outputs | Data validation, provenance tracking |
| **Model Extraction** | Stealing model parameters or architecture | IP theft, competitive advantage loss | Access controls, watermarking |
| **Model Inversion** | Extracting training data from model outputs | Privacy violations | Differential privacy, output filtering |
| **Prompt Injection** | Manipulating prompts to bypass safety measures | Unsafe/incorrect outputs | Input sanitization, prompt hardening |
| **Jailbreaking** | Circumventing AI safety restrictions | Harmful content generation | Safety classifiers, monitoring |
| **API Abuse** | Exploiting AI APIs for unauthorized purposes | Cost overruns, service degradation | Rate limiting, authentication |

## 7. Human-AI Interaction Designer

### 7.1 Role Definition

A Human-AI Interaction Designer creates interfaces and workflows that optimize how humans collaborate with AI systems. They design the "conversation" between humans and AI, ensuring that AI augments human capabilities rather than replacing them.

**Why This Role Emerged:**
- AI systems need thoughtful integration into human workflows
- Poor AI UX leads to low adoption and frustration
- Trust and transparency are critical for AI acceptance
- The design of AI interactions is fundamentally different from traditional UI/UX

### 7.2 Skills Required

**Technical Skills:**
- Understanding of AI capabilities and limitations
- UX research methods for AI systems
- Prototype design for AI interactions
- A/B testing and evaluation methodologies
- Basic understanding of AI model behavior

**Non-Technical Skills:**
- User empathy and observational research
- Design thinking and creative problem-solving
- Cross-functional collaboration
- Communication of complex AI concepts

### 7.3 Salary and Growth

| Level | Salary Range | Experience | Responsibilities |
|-------|-------------|------------|-----------------|
| Junior AI Interaction Designer | $90K-$120K | 0-2 years | Design specific AI interaction patterns |
| AI Interaction Designer | $120K-$160K | 2-5 years | Lead AI UX projects, conduct research |
| Senior AI Interaction Designer | $160K-$210K | 5-8 years | Define AI design standards, mentor teams |
| Head of AI Design | $210K-$280K | 8+ years | Enterprise AI design strategy, team leadership |

**Growth Rate**: 140% (2024-2026)
**Demand**: High — companies realizing AI UX is critical for adoption

### 7.4 Design Principles for Human-AI Interaction

**1. Transparency**
- Show when AI is being used
- Explain AI reasoning when possible
- Make confidence levels visible
- Allow users to see and correct AI assumptions

**2. Control**
- Let users override AI recommendations
- Provide granularity in AI assistance levels
- Allow users to customize AI behavior
- Make it easy to provide feedback

**3. Trust Building**
- Start with high confidence, low stakes suggestions
- Gradually increase AI autonomy as trust builds
- Be honest about AI limitations and errors
- Show improvement over time

**4. Error Recovery**
- Make it easy to identify AI mistakes
- Provide clear paths to correct errors
- Learn from user corrections
- Degrade gracefully when AI fails

**5. Contextual Appropriateness**
- Match AI assistance to task complexity
- Respect user expertise level
- Adapt to user preferences over time
- Consider cultural and contextual factors

```
         ┌→ Agent B1 →┐
```

```
                    ┌→ Specialized Agent A
```

```
        ┌→ Team Lead Agent 1

        │
        └→ Team Lead Agent 2
              └→ Worker Agent 2a
```
Best for: Complex tasks requiring decomposition and coordination

### 13.4 Context Engineering Patterns


```
Context: [Working Memory] [Short-term Memory] [Long-term Memory]
```

```
Context: [Query] → [Retrieved Documents] → [LLM] → [Response]
Strategy: Dynamically retrieve relevant information
Best for: Knowledge-intensive tasks
```

**Pattern 4: Structured Context**
```
Context: [User Profile] + [Task Context] + [Knowledge Base] + [Conversation History]
Strategy: Organized, typed context components
Best for: Enterprise applications with complex context needs
```

- Regulatory requirements may mandate red teaming
- The cost of AI failures is high and growing

### 14.2 Skills Required

- AI model internals and failure modes
- Penetration testing methodologies
- Security tooling and automation
- Code development for attack tools

**Non-Technical Skills:**
- Creative thinking and adversarial mindset
- Documentation and reporting
- Ethical reasoning (attacking for defense)
- Communication of technical risks

## 15. AI Customer Success Manager

### 15.1 Role Definition

An AI Customer Success Manager ensures that customers successfully adopt and derive value from AI products. They combine traditional customer success skills with deep AI knowledge to drive adoption, reduce churn, and expand accounts.

**Why This Role Emerged:**
- AI products require ongoing optimization and support
- Customer success for AI is fundamentally different from traditional SaaS
- AI adoption requires change management and training
- Retention in AI products depends on demonstrating ongoing value

### 15.2 Skills Required

**Technical Skills:**
- Understanding of AI capabilities and use cases
- Data analysis for customer health metrics
- AI product knowledge and troubleshooting
- Implementation and customization knowledge

**Non-Technical Skills:**
- Relationship building and communication
- Change management and training
- Business acumen and ROI analysis
- Problem-solving and creative solutions
