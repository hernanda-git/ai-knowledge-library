## 1. Executive Summary

Artificial intelligence has become the dominant investment category in venture capital. In 2025, AI-related deals accounted for **~38% of all global VC funding** ($115B out of ~$300B total), up from ~12% in 2020. This concentration has reshaped the venture industry: firms that don't have an AI thesis are struggling to raise funds, and AI specialists are raising ever-larger funds.

Key findings for mid-2026:

- **AI VC funding run rate:** ~$136B annualized (2026 H1)
- **AI unicorn births:** ~230 new AI unicorns in 2025, ~120 in H1 2026
- **AI fund formation:** 40% of new VC funds raised in 2025–2026 were AI-focused
- **CVC participation:** Present in 55% of AI mega-rounds ($100M+)
- **Median time to exit:** AI companies are taking 6–8 years to exit (vs. 7–9 for tech overall)
- **Dry powder:** Estimated $80B+ in dedicated AI VC dry powder waiting to be deployed

**The VC landscape is bifurcated:** Generalist firms must have an AI thesis to remain relevant, while specialist AI firms (Air Street, Radical Ventures, Conviction) attract top deal flow by offering deep domain expertise. The next 2–3 years will determine which approach builds the strongest returns.

### 6.2 CVC vs. Traditional VC: Key Differences

| Aspect | Corporate VC | Traditional VC | Best Practice |
|--------|-------------|---------------|--------------|
| **Primary goal** | Strategic alignment (tech access, M&A pipeline, ecosystem) | Financial return (IRR, MOIC, DPI) | Both aligned when startup and corporate share vision |
| **Decision speed** | 4–12 weeks (internal approvals) | 2–6 weeks (GP autonomy) | Startups prefer fast; CVCs losing deals to speed |
| **Check size flexibility** | Limited by corporate budget cycle | Highly flexible | CVCs often co-invest with traditional VCs |
| **Board involvement** | Observer or non-voting | Usually board seat | CVCs increasingly seeking board rights |
| **Path to exit** | Acquisition by parent is common exit | IPO or strategic sale | "M&A exit" is 2x more likely for CVC-backed startups |
| **Downside protection** | Less (can absorb losses for strategy) | More (must show returns to LPs) | CVCs provide patient capital |
| **Valuation discipline** | Can overpay for strategic value | Market-competitive returns | CVCs less price-sensitive |

## 8. SPACs & Alternative Exits

### 8.1 AI SPAC Activity

SPACs (Special Purpose Acquisition Companies) had a boom-and-bust in AI:

| Period | # of AI SPACs | Avg Deal Size | Median Post-SPAC Return (1yr) | Notable Examples |
|--------|--------------|---------------|------------------------------|-----------------|
| 2020–2021 (boom) | 35 | $1.2B | -45% | C3.ai, Wejo, Owlet |
| 2022–2023 (bust) | 8 | $450M | -75% | Most SPACs trading below $2 |
| 2024–2025 (recovery) | 12 | $600M | -10% | Better quality AI companies |
| 2026 (current) | 6 | $800M | TBD | Higher quality targets, better terms |

**Lesson learned:** The 2021 AI SPAC boom was a disaster. Most de-SPAC'd AI companies trade 60–90% below their merger price. Investors are now highly skeptical of SPAC exits for AI companies. Only strong, cash-flow-positive AI businesses can successfully SPAC.

### 8.2 Direct Listings

Direct listings have emerged as a viable alternative for AI companies with strong brand recognition:

- **Advantages:** No dilution, no lock-up period, no SPAC sponsor economics
- **Disadvantages:** No capital raised, price discovery risk
- **AI direct listings:** None to date, but Databricks, Stripe, and Canva are candidates

### 8.3 Secondary Market Exits

Secondary transactions (employees and early investors selling to later-stage investors) have become the primary "partial exit" mechanism for AI startups:

| Year | AI Secondary Volume | Notable Transactions |
|------|--------------------|---------------------|
| 2022 | $4B | Inflection AI ($1.5B secondary) |
| 2023 | $7B | Anthropic ($1B secondary at various points) |
| 2024 | $12B | OpenAI ($4B secondary), Databricks ($2.5B) |
| 2025 | $18B | OpenAI ($10B+ secondary at $80B), Scale AI ($1B) |
| 2026 H1 | $12B | Anthropic ($2B), continuing mega-rounds |

**Key trend:** Secondary markets are becoming a "permanent capital market" for AI unicorns. Top AI companies may never IPO in the traditional sense — instead, they provide liquidity through periodic secondary sales to crossover investors.

## 11. Actionable Insights

### 11.1 For Startup Founders Raising Capital

1. **Raise earlier than you think you need.** The AI fundraising window is compressing. Quality investors make decisions in 1–3 weeks. If you wait too long, you risk missing market timing for your category.

2. **Lead with demonstrable traction, not model quality.** VCs in 2026 care about revenue growth, not perplexity scores. Show you can acquire customers at a reasonable CAC and that your unit economics improve with scale.

3. **Choose investors who can help with distribution.** For AI startups, distribution is the moat, not technology. Investors with enterprise relationships (a16z, Sequoia, Salesforce Ventures) are more valuable than pure financial investors.

4. **Build for a "down-round deflation" scenario.** Many AI companies that raised at peak 2023–2024 valuations will face down rounds. Plan your cap table with anti-dilution protection that encourages, not punishes, realistic repricing.

5. **Consider strategic CVC investment early.** A strategic investment from NVIDIA, Microsoft, or Google can provide compute credits ($100M+ value), distribution partnerships, and credibility that far exceeds the dollar check.

### 11.2 For VCs Building AI Portfolios

1. **Don't overpay for the "AI premium."** The valuation disconnect between AI and non-AI companies at similar revenue stages cannot persist indefinitely. Companies valued at 40x ARR with 55% gross margins will face compression when growth inevitably decelerates.

2. **Build AI expertise internally.** The most successful AI investors of the next decade will be those who can technically evaluate AI companies, not those who invest based on founder brand alone. Hire PhDs, ex-founders, and AI researchers onto investment teams.

3. **Prepare for a "great consolidation."** The AI startup market will see significant consolidation in 2027–2029. Position your portfolio to be acquirers (strong balance sheets) or have clear strategic fit with potential acquirers.

4. **Focus on durable differentiation.** Ask: "What prevents a well-funded Google/Bridgewater/McKinsey team from building this in 6 months?" If the answer is "our proprietary model," you don't have a moat.

5. **Geographic diversification is underpriced.** European, Indian, and Southeast Asian AI companies have lower valuations, less competition, and comparable founder quality. The ROI per dollar deployed is often better outside the SF bubble.

### 11.3 For LPs Allocating to AI

1. **AI VC is a "tournament" market.** Top-tier AI allocators (a16z, Sequoia) have access to the best deal flow. New AI-focused funds with differentiated theses can also generate alpha. Middle-tier generalist funds pretending to be AI-savvy will underperform.

2. **Seek funds with operational AI expertise.** LPs should favor GPs who have built and scaled AI products, not just invested in them. AI is a technology category where operator-domain expertise matters more than financial engineering.

3. **Secondary market exposure is an option.** Instead of committing to a 10-year fund, consider secondary AI purchases (buying stakes from employees or early investors) which offer more liquidity and lower J-curve risk.

4. **Watch the "inference cost trap."** AI companies whose unit economics depend on falling inference costs are vulnerable. If compute prices stabilize or rise (due to energy costs or demand), many AI business models break.

5. **AI is not a sector — it's a transformation.** The best AI investments may not be labeled "AI companies." Incumbents that successfully embed AI (Microsoft, Salesforce, ServiceNow) may generate better risk-adjusted returns than pure-play AI startups.
