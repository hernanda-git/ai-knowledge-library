# 01 — AI Regulation & Antitrust: Global Landscape Overview (2026)

## 1. Introduction: The Regulatory Tipping Point

Artificial intelligence entered 2026 as the most heavily regulated emergent technology in modern history. The convergence of three forces — frontier model capabilities, widespread enterprise deployment, and a cascade of legislative actions across every major economy — has transformed AI governance from a niche policy concern into a central axis of geopolitical competition, corporate strategy, and fundamental rights protection.

### 1.1 Why 2026 Is the Watershed Year

Five factors distinguish 2026 from prior years in AI regulation:

1. **Legal enforceability**: The EU AI Act's substantive provisions took full effect in August 2025, with enforcement actions beginning in Q1 2026. This marks the first time a comprehensive, binding AI regulatory framework with extraterritorial reach has been operational.
2. **Antitrust awakening**: Competition authorities in the US, UK, EU, and China launched coordinated investigations into AI market concentration. The Amazon-Anthropic investigation (716-part inquiry), DOJ scrutiny of Microsoft-OpenAI, and CMA's probe into foundation model partnerships signal a paradigm shift.
3. **Export control escalation**: Following the October 2023 and March 2024 BIS semiconductor rules, 2025-2026 saw controls extended to model weights, cloud computing services, and AI training infrastructure, creating a de facto bifurcation of the global AI supply chain.
4. **State-level activism**: In the absence of comprehensive US federal legislation, states — Colorado, California, New York, Texas, and others — enacted their own AI laws, creating a patchwork with significant compliance burdens.
5. **International coordination attempts**: Despite geopolitical tensions, multilateral bodies (OECD, GPAI, UN, Council of Europe) produced binding instruments and non-binding principles that shape national legislation.

### 1.2 The Four Pillars of AI Governance

We conceptualize the global AI regulatory landscape around four interconnected pillars:

| Pillar | Focus | Key Instruments |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| **Risk-Based Regulation** | Categorize AI systems by risk level, impose proportionate obligations | EU AI Act, Canada AIDA, Brazil Bill 2338 |
| **Competition/Antitrust** | Prevent market concentration, ensure contestability | Section 1/2 Sherman Act, EU TFEU Art. 101/102, CMA investigations |
| **National Security/Trade** | Control dual-use AI capabilities, protect critical infrastructure | BIS Export Controls, CFIUS reviews, Wassenaar Arrangement |
| **Fundamental Rights** | Protect privacy, non-discrimination, due process | GDPR, EU AI Act Title IV, state anti-bias laws |

## 2. The Global Regulatory Landscape by Jurisdiction

### 2.1 European Union: First Mover with Teeth

The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689), adopted June 13, 2024, entered into force August 1, 2024, with phased implementation through August 2027. It represents the world's first comprehensive horizontal AI regulation.

**Core architecture:**
- Four-tier risk classification: Unacceptable (prohibited), High-risk (regulated), Limited-risk (transparency), Minimal-risk (unregulated)
- Extra-territorial scope: Applies to providers and deployers established outside the EU if their AI system's output is used in the EU
- Penalties: Up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover (whichever higher) for prohibited practices; up to €15 million or 3% for other violations
- Governance: European AI Office (within European Commission), European Artificial Intelligence Board (national regulators), Scientific Panel of Independent Experts

**Implementation timeline (accelerated):**
- February 2, 2025: Prohibited practices provisions took effect
- August 2, 2025: GPAI transparency obligations, codes of practice
- August 2, 2026: Annex III high-risk systems (standards-dependent)
- August 2, 2027: Annex II high-risk systems (as notified standards)

**Key developments in 2025-2026:**
- First enforcement action against a foundation model provider (March 2026)
- Publication of harmonized standards by CEN/CENELEC (JTC 21)
- First codes of practice for General Purpose AI adopted
- Member state designation of national competent authorities

### 2.2 United States: Sectoral Patchwork with Federal Aspirations

The US approach remains fundamentally sectoral, with federal agencies regulating AI within existing statutory authorities.

**Federal executive action:**
- Executive Order 14110 (October 30, 2023): Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy AI
- OMB Memorandum M-24-10: Agency AI use requirements
- 2025 follow-on EO expanding federal procurement rules
- AI.gov and National AI Initiative Office coordination

**Federal legislative prospects (2025-2026):**
- Bipartisan Framework for AI Act (discussion draft)
- SAFE Innovation Act (border security AI)
- Algorithmic Accountability Act (reintroduced)
- Federal AI Risk Management Act (NIST standards-based)

**State-level activism (the dominant regulatory force in 2026):**
- **Colorado AI Act** (SB 24-205): Enacted May 2024, effective 2026. First US state comprehensive AI law. Requires risk assessments for high-risk AI systems, consumer notice, opportunity to correct. Covers algorithmic discrimination in employment, housing, education, healthcare, financial services.
- **California SB 1047** (Safe and Secure Innovation for Frontier AI Models Act): Passed September 2024, effective 2026. Requires safety testing for frontier models (training compute >10^26 FLOPs), whistleblower protections, emergency shutdown capability, annual third-party audits. Created CalCompute public cloud.
- **New York** Executive Order 30 (state agency AI use), NY AI Law (employment discrimination, passed 2025)
- **Texas** AI Safety Act (2025), focusing on high-risk systems in critical infrastructure
- **Illinois** AI Transparency Act (2025), hiring AI disclosure
- **Maryland** AI Bias Testing Law (employment AI, 2026 effective date)

### 2.3 China: State-Led, Security-First, Rapidly Evolving

China's AI governance model is distinct: top-down, party-state directed, focused on social stability, content control, and technological sovereignty.

**Regulatory framework (cumulative by 2026):**
- **Algorithm Regulation Provisions** (2022): Recommendation algorithm registration, transparency requirements
- **Deep Synthesis Provisions** (2023): AI-generated content labeling (水印), content moderation, training data compliance
- **Generative AI Interim Measures** (2023): Mandatory safety reviews, training data compliance with socialist core values, content filters, user age verification
- **Draft AI Law** (2024): Comprehensive framework, expected finalization 2025-2026
- **Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL)** (2021): Cross-border data transfer restrictions affecting AI training
- **Data Security Law** (2021): Data classification, national security reviews
- **Cybersecurity Law** (2017, amended): Content controls, critical information infrastructure protections

**Key characteristics:**
- Algorithm registration with Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC)
- Mandatory safety assessment for generative AI before public release
- "Socialist core values" compliance in training data and outputs
- National AI safety review board (2025 creation)
- Export controls on AI chips reciprocating US restrictions
- Push for domestic AI ecosystem (Kunlun, Huawei Ascend, Cambricon chips)

### 2.4 Other Major Jurisdictions

**United Kingdom:** Pro-innovation, light-touch approach. 2023 AI Safety Summit (Bletchley Park). AI Safety Institute established. 2025 AI Regulation Bill (sector-led, principles-based). No single AI regulator; existing regulators (ICO, CMA, Ofcom) enforce AI in their domains.

**Canada:** Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA), part of Bill C-27. Risk-based, similar to EU tiered approach. Proposed AI and Data Commissioner. Passed House of Commons December 2024, Senate review ongoing.

**Japan:** Soft law approach. AI Guidelines for Business (2024). AI Strategy Council (2025). Focus on economic growth and societal benefit. No comprehensive AI law as of 2026, but active in G7 Hiroshima Process and non-binding principles.

**South Korea:** AI Act (passed December 2024, effective 2026). Similar EU-style tiered risk approach. Requirements for high-risk AI, transparency, human oversight. AI Safety Institute (2025).

**Brazil:** Bill No. 2338/2023 (comprehensive AI regulation). Risk-based classification, algorithmic impact assessments, transparency, accountability. Similar to EU AI Act with stronger consumer protections. Parliamentary debate ongoing.

**India:** No comprehensive AI law. Ministry of Electronics and IT (MeitY) advisory approach. 2024 AI Governance Guidelines (draft, industry self-regulation emphasis). Focus on AI for development (AI for All). Data Protection Act 2023 applies indirectly.

**Singapore:** Model AI Governance Framework (updated 2024). Testing framework (AI Verify). Light-touch, innovation-friendly. Emphasis on voluntary standards and international interoperability.

## 3. The Antitrust-AI Nexus

### 3.1 Why AI Markets Raise Competition Concerns

The structure of AI markets exhibits several features that trigger antitrust scrutiny:

1. **Hyper-concentration**: The foundation model market is dominated by a handful of firms. As of 2026, the top 3 model providers (OpenAI, Google/DeepMind, Anthropic) command >85% of the frontier model market.
2. **Vertical integration**: Big tech firms control multiple layers: cloud infrastructure (AWS, Azure, GCP), training hardware (NVIDIA GPUs, Google TPUs), model development, and application distribution.
3. **Strategic investments**: Major cloud providers have invested billions in AI startups — Microsoft in OpenAI ($13B+), Amazon in Anthropic ($8B), Google in Anthropic ($2B+) — creating complex inter-corporate relationships that may reduce competitive independence.
4. **GPU bottleneck**: NVIDIA controls >80% of the AI training GPU market (H100, B200). Supply allocation decisions effectively determine who can compete in frontier AI.
5. **Data access moats**: Large-scale training data, user feedback data, and proprietary datasets create barriers to entry.
6. **Talent concentration**: The AI research talent market is extremely tight, with top firms hoarding PhD researchers.

### 3.2 Major Investigations and Cases (2025-2026)

**Anthropic S-1 Filing and Amazon Investigation:**
- Anthropic's confidential S-1 filing (late 2025) revealed its governance structure and Amazon relationship
- Amazon's $8B total investment includes AWS credits convertible to equity at Amazon's discretion
- 716-part regulatory inquiry (led by UK CMA with EU and DOJ cooperation) into Amazon-Anthropic relationship
- Concerns: Amazon may use Anthropic models exclusively for AWS, limiting competition; AWS credits create vendor lock-in; board seats and information rights give Amazon control without formal acquisition
- Potential remedies: Divestiture of Amazon's stake, "open access" requirements for AWS, firewalls between Amazon retail and AI services

**Microsoft-OpenAI Investigation:**
- DOJ and FTC joint investigation into Microsoft's $13B+ investment in OpenAI
- Issues: Did Microsoft effectively acquire OpenAI through convertible debt? Board observer rights after OpenAI's November 2023 governance crisis? Azure exclusive cloud provider lock-in?
- UK CMA: Initial merger assessment found Microsoft-OpenAI partnership does not qualify as a merger (Section 33 Enterprise Act), but ongoing monitoring

**Google-Antitrust:**
- DOJ v. Google (monopoly maintenance in search): AI overviews, Gemini integration in search may constitute exclusionary conduct
- EU Digital Markets Act: Google's AI services (Gemini, AI Overviews) as core platform services
- Google's $2B investment in Anthropic under scrutiny

**NVIDIA Antitrust:**
- DOJ investigation into NVIDIA's GPU sales practices (alleged bundling, discriminatory access, allocation favoring certain customers)
- French Autorité de la Concurrence raided NVIDIA offices (2024)
- EU informal investigation into NVIDIA supply allocation
- Claims of tying CUDA software to GPU hardware to lock in developers

**OpenAI Restructuring:**
- OpenAI's transition from non-profit to for-profit (2024-2026)
- Regulatory review of whether the non-profit mission can be enforced post-restructuring
- Antitrust concerns about OpenAI's exclusive distribution deals (Apple partnership, enterprise agreements)

### 3.3 The "Compute as a Bottleneck" Problem

A unique feature of AI antitrust is that the key input — compute — is controlled by a single company (NVIDIA) whose allocation decisions determine competitive outcomes. This has spawned new legal theories:

- **Essential facilities doctrine applied to compute**: If GPU access is essential for competition in AI markets and not reasonably duplicable, NVIDIA may have a duty to deal on non-discriminatory terms
- **Input foreclosure**: Cloud providers who integrate AI services may deny competitors access to compute or grant preferential access to affiliated model developers
- **Cross-subsidization**: Cloud profits subsidize AI model development, enabling predatory pricing in downstream AI markets

## 4. Export Controls and Technology Security

### 4.1 The US-China Tech War's AI Dimension

AI export controls have become the primary instrument of US technology security policy toward China:

**Semiconductor controls:**
- October 7, 2022: Initial BIS rule restricting advanced chips to China
- October 17, 2023: Expanded rule lowering performance thresholds, adding chip-to-chip connectivity limits (bandwidth controls)
- March 29, 2024: Further refinement, closing loopholes (e.g., chips designed for China specifically below thresholds)
- May 2025: Additional restrictions on AI memory bandwidth, packaging technologies
- December 2025: Performance density thresholds extended to fintech ASICs

**Model weight controls:**
- January 2025: BIS proposed rule requiring export licenses for model weights of dual-use foundation models
- June 2025: Final rule effective; threshold based on model performance benchmarks
- August 2025: International partnership on weight controls (US, Japan, Netherlands, South Korea)

**Cloud computing controls:**
- 2024: Proposed regulation making US cloud providers responsible for foreign access to AI training compute
- 2025 "Know Your Customer" cloud rules: Providers must verify identity of training customers, report foreign-of-concern training runs
- 2026: Enforcement actions against cloud providers for Chinese entities accessing restricted compute

### 4.2 Allied Coordination

- **Wassenaar Arrangement** 2024 plenary: Agreed to add AI-specific dual-use controls
- **Chip 4 Alliance** (US, Japan, Netherlands, South Korea): Export control coordination
- **AI Safety Institutes**: US AISI, UK AISI, Japan AISI, EU AI Office coordination on model evaluation standards
- **Investment screening**: CFIUS reviews of AI investments for national security risks; reciprocal schemes in EU (FDI Screening Regulation), UK (NSI Act)

## 5. International Governance Frameworks

### 5.1 Multilateral Principles and Processes

| Instrument | Type | Status | Key Provisions |
|------------|------|--------|----------------|
| OECD AI Principles (2019, updated 2024) | Soft law | Adopted by 47 countries | Inclusive growth, human-centered, transparency, robustness, accountability |
| G7 Hiroshima AI Process (2023) | Political commitment | Ongoing | International Guiding Principles, Code of Conduct for Advanced AI Systems |
| Council of Europe AI Convention (2024) | Binding treaty | Opened for signature Sep 2024 | Human rights, democracy, rule of law throughout AI lifecycle |
| UNESCO Recommendation on AI Ethics (2021) | Soft law | 193 member states | Ethical framework, proportionality, do no harm |
| UN AI Advisory Body (2023) | Expert body | Interim report June 2024 | Global AI governance architecture proposal |
| Bletchley Declaration (2023) | Political declaration | AI Safety Summit | Frontier AI risks, international cooperation |
| Seoul Summit (2024) | Political summit | Follow-up to Bletchley | Safety commitments, transparency pledges |
| ISO/IEC 42001 (2023) | Standard | Published Dec 2023 | AI management system standard, certifiable |

### 5.2 The Governance Gap

Despite this proliferation of frameworks, significant gaps remain:

1. **No binding global AI treaty**: The Council of Europe Convention is regional (primarily European signatories). There is no equivalent of the Paris Agreement or UNFCCC for AI.
2. **Enforcement asymmetry**: EU has strong enforcement; US has fragmented enforcement; many countries have no enforcement capacity.
3. **Geopolitical fragmentation**: The US-China technology rivalry prevents consensus on threat models and regulatory approaches.
4. **Regulatory arbitrage**: Companies can locate high-risk AI activities in jurisdictions with weakest regulation.
5. **Fast-moving target**: Regulation lags capability development by 12-24 months. By the time rules take effect, the technology has evolved.
6. **Compute governance**: No international framework exists for monitoring or controlling AI compute resources cross-border.

## 7. Key Themes Across Documents

1. **Regulatory divergence vs. convergence**: While the EU, US, and China have fundamentally different regulatory philosophies, there is convergence on certain points: risk-based approaches, transparency requirements, human oversight, and bias testing. Divergence remains sharp on content moderation, data sovereignty, and national security controls.

2. **The compliance burden**: A company deploying AI globally in 2026 must comply with potentially overlapping regulatory regimes. Document alignment and cross-jurisdictional compliance is a major operational challenge.

3. **Antitrust as an AI governance tool**: Competition enforcement is emerging as a de facto AI regulatory mechanism where specific AI laws don't exist. Antitrust actions shape market structure, determine who can develop frontier models, and influence safety outcomes.

4. **The compute-control thesis**: The ability to train frontier models is determined by access to compute (GPUs, cloud infrastructure, energy). Regulation of compute — through export controls, antitrust remedies, and safety requirements — is the most powerful leverage point for AI governance.

5. **Democratization vs. centralization**: Open-weight models (Meta Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek) push toward democratization of AI capabilities, while frontier model regulation and compute controls push toward centralization. This tension will define the next phase of AI governance.
